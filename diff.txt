> - Core Premise: On the night a luxury underwater resort opens four hundred and twelve feet below the Atlantic off Cape Hatteras, a Cold War submarine clash punches an invisible wound through the building, and the men with the power to evacuate everyone inside it would rather let the ocean close over the truth than let the truth surface.
> - Story Promise: A glittering monument to American confidence dies one chamber at a time while the people inside negotiate, again and again, the trade-off between survival, decency, and the truth — and the book treats every trade-off as binding.
> - Reader Promise: The paranoid intimacy of a sealed-environment thriller, the engineering dread of slow structural failure, and the moral vertigo of an ensemble forced to make impossible choices with the ocean pressing on the glass — an elegant, period-faithful disaster epic that reads like a lost 1979 hardcover.
> - Ending Promise: The hotel goes down on the page. Some characters are saved; some pay for the saving. A sacrifice is earned, not assigned. The cover-up is wounded but not yet defeated, and the survivors walk out of the book carrying a debt the institutions on the surface have not yet been forced to acknowledge.

> The narration sits in close third, one character per scene, anchored to that character's nervous system without head-hopping. Section breaks are the only place point of view changes; the reader always knows whose body they are inside. Scenes breathe in the calm of opening night and snap shut around mechanical failure or new bad news; chapters earn their stillness by paying for it later. Every chapter should leave a residue — a sensory image, a moral cost, a relationship altered — and that residue should be retrievable by motif before it is retrievable by plot.

> The book grants the reader low ambiguity about what is physically happening (the building is failing; the submersibles are limited; the surface is delaying) and high ambiguity about who is responsible for the moral chain that put the building, the submarines, and the cover-up into the same waters on the same night. There is no single villain who absolves the rest, and no single hero who exonerates the system. The Aurelia is photographed in glamour and then in pressure red; the sea outside is rendered without personification; the dialogue carries subtext as a structural load and only collapses to plain language when crisis earns the line.
> 
> Period restraint over modern shock. The book remembers it is set in 1979 and trusts the reader to do the work — no on-the-nose modern parallels, no anachronistic technology, no contemporary vocabulary smuggled in for accessibility. Grief, when it comes, is shown rather than narrated; sentimentality is treated as a craft failure.

> - The hotel cannot be saved. The story is about who survives the loss, not whether the building does. Any plot move that suggests structural rescue is a violation of the contract.
> - Physics is honored. Pressure, depth, decompression times, breathable atmosphere, sonar range, and the limits of 1970s submersible engineering are real constraints. No character escapes by violating them; every escape is paid for in time, light, breath, or a life.
> - The Aurelia sits at 412 feet (~125 m) below the Atlantic surface off Cape Hatteras. The number is fixed; ascent times, decompression windows, sonar range, hull pressure, sub-cycle times, and visibility from the surface all respect it. No prose may drift to "five hundred feet" or "the deep" without this anchor in mind.
> - The sea is indifferent. There is no malevolent ocean; sharks and scavengers are atmospheric presence, never plot-bearing antagonists. The Soviet wreck attracts scavengers; that is texture, not climax.
> - Institutions protect themselves before people. The Navy, the developer, and the press will, given a choice, protect their reputations over a single life — until specific human beings inside those institutions break with that choice and pay personally for the breaking.
> - Information has cost. Every reveal is paid for in lives, oxygen, light, time, or trust. The reader never receives a free truth.
> - No deus ex machina. No surprise hardware, no surprise rescue ship, no surprise super-engineer, no eleventh-hour Soviet-American friendship gesture. The best ending the characters can earn is the only ending available.
> - One POV per scene, close third only, no omniscient narrator, no head-hopping. The narrator does not know things the POV character does not know.
> - The cover-up is real and mostly successful. The book wounds it; it does not defeat it. Any ending in which a single journalist's tape brings down the entire institutional apparatus is a violation.

> The Aurelia is, by design, an ensemble novel — but a strong novel needs a structural protagonist whose interior contradiction can carry the book's spine. Erik Halvorsen, the architect, is that spine. He built the thing that is killing them, he warned about exactly the failure they are now living through, he was overruled, and he is wearing the third memo in his coat pocket while the orchestra plays. Tomás Reyes, the chief engineer, is the equal-weight ally who anchors the operational reality, but the architect carries the moral fulcrum: he is the only person aboard who knew the failure was possible before the failure happened, and the only person whose name is on the building. Choosing the architect over the engineer keeps the central interior question — *was my silence a form of authorship?* — at the center of every chapter. Every other major ensemble character is a foil to that question.
