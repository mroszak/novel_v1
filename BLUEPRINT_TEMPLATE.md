## Market Promise
Optional but recommended. The commercial spine the rest of the pipeline reads. Spec, draft, judge, and audit all see these fields. Empty/absent sections are fine; the engine works with empty contracts.

- Reader Avatar: One sentence describing who buys this book and why. Be specific (age, taste, time-of-day reading habit), not aspirational. Example: "A reader who finishes a book in two airport sittings and tells two friends before landing."
- Shelf / Comps: Two-to-five exact books, films, or series this sits beside on the buyer's mental shelf. Use these as quality and tradition references. Example: "Hunt for Red October, The Abyss, The Poseidon Adventure."
- Core Commercial Hook: The one-sentence reason someone clicks "buy." A specific premise, a specific danger, a specific promise. Example: "A luxury underwater resort's grand opening becomes a sealed deathtrap after a buried Cold War incident ruptures the structure."
- Trope Stack: The genre expectations being deliberately satisfied. List 3-6. Example: "sealed-environment disaster; military secret bleeds into the present; ordinary people forced into competence under pressure."
- Freshness Angle: What makes this not feel generic. The one or two specific choices that differentiate it from the comp shelf. Example: "The architecture of the failure is military, not natural. The hotel was built on top of an unfinished war."
- Pacing Contract: How fast the book needs to move for this market. One sentence on the rhythm the reader is buying. Example: "Slow-burn pressure with violent ruptures; the first death lands by the end of chapter 3."
- Emotional Promise: The feeling the reader is buying. One or two sentences. Example: "Elegant spectacle collapsing into trapped survival, where every choice costs something the reader watches the protagonist lose."
- Cover/Blurb Keywords: Terms to influence packaging later. Reserved by the engine; not used in current generation but recorded with the manifest.
  - Replace with a concrete keyword
  - Replace with another concrete keyword
- Series Potential: Standalone, series-starter, or trilogy obligation. One sentence on what the ending must leave open or close. Example: "Standalone with a survivor whose final image suggests a sequel without requiring one."
- Chapter-Level Retention Strategy: Per chapter-function reader job. Each function gets a declared reader job that the spec, draft, judge, and audit all read. Failure to honor the declared job is a flag.
  - opening: make the premise irresistible
  - early-escalation: prove the danger is real
  - midpoint: change what the reader thinks the story is
  - late-escalation: make escape or truth feel impossible
  - climax: pay off pressure, sacrifice, and revelation in a single sustained beat
  - aftermath: leave an emotional scar or a series hook the reader cannot stop thinking about

## Continuity Manifest
Optional but recommended. The structural spine. Object state, spatial geography, timeline order, reveal sequence, relationship intensities, and motif evolution that the engine reads chapter by chapter to keep continuity from drifting past chapter 4-5. All fields optional; an empty manifest means "engine carries no structured memory" and behavior is identical to a pre-manifest blueprint.

### Persistent Objects
List concrete physical or informational objects whose state matters across chapters. Each entry: name, current state, who possesses or controls it, last seen chapter (use 0 for "before chapter 1"). Format: `name | state | possessor | last-seen-chapter`.
- Object 1 | initial state | possessor or location | 0
- Object 2 | initial state | possessor or location | 0

### Spatial Registry
Named spaces and rooms that recur, with their default access rules and current state. Each entry: name, what it is, who can be there, current condition. Format: `name | description | access | condition`.
- Space 1 | brief description | who can be there | normal
- Space 2 | brief description | who can be there | normal

### Timeline Anchors
Named time anchors used to keep chapter-to-chapter chronology coherent. Each entry: anchor label, what it marks, optional offset from a baseline (`T+0:00`, `T+1:30`, etc). Format: `label | description | offset`.
- T+0:00 | story begins | baseline
- T+0:30 | first scene close | +0:30 from start

### Reveal Schedule
The intended order in which information becomes available to specific characters and to the reader. One entry per planned reveal: thread, who learns it, when (chapter number), how (show / hint / reveal / payoff). Format: `thread | learner | chapter | mode`.
- Reveal A | reader | 2 | hint
- Reveal A | protagonist | 4 | reveal
- Reveal B | reader | 3 | hint

### Relationship States
Per character pairing: current trust, current distance, current dependency, current rivalry. Use 0-100 or qualitative shorthand. Format: `pair | trust | distance | dependency | rivalry`.
- Protagonist x Antagonist | trust=10 | distance=high | dependency=low | rivalry=high
- Protagonist x Supporting | trust=70 | distance=close | dependency=medium | rivalry=low

### Motif States
For each motif from the Motif/Symbol Bank: current intensity, last appearance chapter, current evolution stage (introduced / recurring / inverted / paid off). Format: `motif | intensity | last-chapter | stage`.
- Motif 1 | low | 0 | introduced
- Motif 2 | low | 0 | introduced
