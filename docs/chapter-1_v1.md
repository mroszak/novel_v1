# Chapter One

# The Aurelia

The pod shivered on its cable. Erik Halvorsen kept his palm flat against the brass rail and let the tremor pass into the bones of his wrist, the way a sailor takes the deck and not the rail to know his sea. Through the porthole there was nothing to see but black water moving around its own reflection of him: a tall man in evening wool, hair gone to gunmetal at the temples, an unopened invitation curled in one gloved hand. The depth needle behind the operator's elbow ticked past three hundred and sixty feet and went on settling. The cable hummed two notes apart, the lower note steadier than the higher, the way a tuning fork hums when the room is cold.

Beyond the porthole the dark turned milky. A field of sodium lamps lifted out of the deeper black, then a second field, then the dome itself: an enormous lit jellyfish shouldering up through its own shadow, ribbed in steel and skinned in glass, and around it a pale halo where the floodlights touched the suspended particulate and made it shine. The Aurelia. He had drawn it on a yellow legal pad in 1974 in a one-room office in Oslo and crossed out the dome twice before he believed it. Above the dome, on the upper deck, one suite stood unlit — a single dark aperture in a building otherwise burning, the windows blank as a closed eye, the acrylic ceiling of that crown room reflecting only the lamps below it. The cable carried the pod down past it without ceremony. "Four-twelve, gentlemen," the operator said, and the pod kissed its cradle with a long, soft, engineered sigh.

The lock cycled. Polished walnut, brass, a steward who took the gloves from his hand without seeming to take them, a porter's bow that was not quite a bow, an older man buttoning his blazer over his waistcoat — *Mr. Halvorsen, welcome down, sir,* in the tone hotels reserve for people whose names are on the masthead — and then the second door opened, and the Atrium took the breath out of him before he could decide whether to give it. Three stories of glass and air rose into a dome the diameter of a small chapel, and the floodlights laid on the outside of that dome turned the water above into a slow, blue, moving ceiling, and through that ceiling fish passed in single, casual file as though they had been hired for the evening. The helical staircase climbed in a single unbroken curve to the upper deck. The orchestra was playing something restrained and Italian under it. Champagne, perfume, cigarette smoke, the warm wool smell of a hundred dinner jackets pressed in cedar that morning — it all met him at the threshold like weather, and a polite, scattered round of applause moved across the room as people who had been told who he was tilted their heads to look.

He counted, briefly and out of habit, the lamps in the dome ribs. Sixteen. Two banks of eight. The number was correct.

He shifted in his coat. The packet inside the breast pocket pressed against his ribs the way a wallet does, except that he had stopped feeling his wallet when he was twenty-four and he had not stopped feeling this. Habit, he told himself. The way an old fracture announces itself before rain. He drew his glove off and touched the lining flat with the back of his hand to make the stiffness lie down, and it lay down, and the cable hum behind him faded into the music of the dome.

Past the concierge's polished walnut desk he caught, without looking for it, the routine equipment status board: a small black slate in a brass frame, kept up in white grease pencil. *DAPHNE — moored, Cradle 2. TRITON — moored, Cradle 3. NEREID — moored, Cradle 4, scheduled inspection 06:00.* Someone's thumb had smudged the *e* in *Nereid*. He registered the names the way one registers the line of taxis at a hotel rank — present, expected, unremarkable — and walked past.

"Erik."

Across the floor, by the foot of the staircase, Roland Vauclair was waiting.

---

Vauclair came through the crowd without seeming to part it, the way good hosts move. He was a tall man going gracefully heavy, his white tie set at the angle French men set white ties, his hair brushed back from a forehead the magazine photographers had been kind to. He took Erik's hand in both of his.

"You came," he said, in the voice he used on investors: the voice that made everyone in earshot remember that they had been chosen.

"I said I would."

"You said many things in the last year, my friend."

The crowd was a half step away. Erik kept his face composed.

"It's a lovely room, Roland."

"It is your room."

"It is the room we drew."

Vauclair's smile broadened a degree, and a degree was the difference between charming and dangerous in him; Erik had learned to mark it.

"Come," Vauclair said. "Let me introduce you to people who have already decided they admire you."

A girl in service black went past with a tray of crystal and stumbled, not badly, on the brass strip where the carpet ended. The tray rocked. A flute went over and a thin spray of champagne tipped down the front of her apron and onto the marble. Her face did the small awful thing service faces do — the calculation of how much trouble — and Vauclair, mid-stride, mid-sentence, simply turned his free hand and caught the tray's lower lip before the second flute went, and steadied it, and handed her his pocket square.

"Nothing," he said to her quietly. "I spilled my own once at the Élysée. They made me a Chevalier anyway."

"Sir —"

"Go on, dear. Use the back stair. Ask the headwaiter for a fresh apron and tell him I sent you; he likes to feel useful."

She went. Vauclair was already turning back to Erik as though nothing had occurred, his hand finding Erik's elbow, and Erik, against his will, remembered why he had once gotten on a plane to Paris on three days' notice to argue about a column thickness with this man over a bottle of Margaux. The memory did not improve anything. It only made it harder to carry.

"Mr. Halvorsen?"

A woman with a press badge and pearls had stepped politely into their line. A photographer was a pace behind her, lens cap on. Vauclair did not turn his head; his hand tightened a fraction at Erik's elbow.

"A question for Mr. Halvorsen, if I may, Mr. Vauclair."

"Of course," Vauclair said.

"Mr. Halvorsen, you've been notably absent from the Aurelia's publicity for the last eight months. The press notes have been calling you the building's co-author. Some of us were beginning to wonder if there had been —" she smiled, the practiced smile of someone used to making questions sound harmless — "creative differences."

He breathed out through his nose. He counted, in the privacy behind his teeth, four. Then he said, "The building is the building. It speaks for itself."

"And your absence?"

"I had other work."

"Mr. Vauclair," she said, sliding past him, "would you call Mr. Halvorsen the building's co-author?"

"I would call him," Vauclair said warmly, "the man without whom the Aurelia is not the Aurelia. There is no co- about it. He is its author. I am only its impresario."

Her pencil moved. The photographer raised the lens. Erik held his face still for the flash and did not look at Vauclair.

"Roland," he said, when she had gone, "don't."

"Don't what."

"Don't hand me the building in front of a journalist."

"I handed you nothing. I corrected an error of fact."

"Roland."

"My friend." Vauclair turned at last to look at him, and his eyes were warm and entirely unsurprised, and the French came up out of him the way it did when he was managing a difficult horse. "Tu es venu. C'est bien. Ce soir, on est ensemble. The rest is — *winter housekeeping*. Yes? Come."

He drew Erik onward into the crush, and Erik, having no graceful way to refuse, went.

---

At the long zinc bar on the west arc of the Atrium, Felix Crane set down a glass of something amber he had not asked for and let the cold of the brass rail come up through his palm. He had been at this work long enough to know when a room had a story in it and when a room only had a party. The Aurelia, on first pass, had both, and the trick was deciding which one *Continental* was going to pay for.

The class portrait assembled itself around him without effort. Investors at the railing in clusters of three, the way investors always stood — never two, never four. The Florida senator at the foot of the stair, his wife in green, his aide a step behind with a folded program. A clutch of NOAA scientists in better suits than scientists usually wore, hands behind their backs, looking up at the dome as if it were a colleague. A retired admiral in evening dress, two stars on the breast and a face like a boarded window, standing alone near the orchestra, one ear cocked toward — Crane registered it and let it go — a pod-system speaker on the column behind him, from which a clipped, unintelligible scrap of radio chatter had briefly leaked before the steward at the panel reached up and adjusted the volume off. Restricted-water nothing, Crane decided. Coast Guard inshore. The admiral's face did not change.

The Marchettis came down the stair together as if they had been doing this for decades: he small and exact in a dinner jacket whose lapels belonged to another era, she on his arm in a column of dark blue. At her throat, against the blue, a small silver pendant the size of a child's thumbnail caught the dome light and threw it back — a lifeboat, oar to oar — and Crane noted it the way he noted such things, and let his eye move on.

The Aldridges stood near the orchestra, the husband's hand at the small of the wife's back, the way young marrieds stand when they are still surprised to be allowed. Crane catalogued them and moved on.

Inside his jacket the recorder rested against his ribs, off, the rectangular weight of it equal to the wallet on the other side, the way a tailor likes a coat to balance. He had loaded a fresh sixty-minute cassette in the cab from the airfield. He had palmed the spare into the inner pocket. He had not pressed *record*. The Vauclair quote he wanted would not arrive before the toast. There was no point burning tape on a room.

He took his pencil from the inside of his cuff and wrote *creative differences* on the cocktail napkin. He had heard the question across the room, and he had seen Halvorsen's face go very still under it, and he had seen Vauclair's hand on the architect's elbow. *Co-author*, he wrote underneath, and put a question mark next to it, and then crossed it out and put two.

He folded the napkin into the steno notebook, slid the notebook into his hip pocket, and let his hand brush the recorder once on the way back to the brass rail. Later. After the toast. After Vauclair had said the line he was going to say about American ambition and the deep and the future.

The orchestra moved into something with a Verdi feel in the strings. Crane lifted his glass to no one in particular and watched.

---

On the upper deck, Adriana Vauclair walked the curve of the corridor with her clipboard held in the crook of her arm. The carpet up here was thicker and the music thinner; the orchestra came up through the floor in registrations rather than notes. Her heels made no sound. She passed three suites with the doors propped, turn-down done, the chocolates centered on the pillows, the little card placed at twelve o'clock with the guest's name. Routine. She made three checkmarks in pencil along the right margin.

At the end of the corridor she stopped and used the staff key.

The Coral Suite opened with the hush of a well-fitted door. She did not step in. She did not need to. From the threshold she could see what she had come to see: the bed turned down, the silver bucket in its stand sweating evenly, the unopened bottle still wearing its foil. The tray of canapés on the writing desk had been refreshed at five and would be refreshed again at nine. Above the bed, where any other suite would have had a ceiling, there was instead the four-centimeter acrylic and beyond it the unlit Atlantic, a black in which one could not quite tell what was water and what was the room's own dark. The floodlamps on the dome ribs threw nothing up here. She looked at the dark for a count of three and made a small mark on her clipboard: *CS — ready, 7:22 p.m.*

A footstep behind her. Her father's cologne reached her before he did.

"I thought I might find you up here."

"You always think that, and you are always right."

"My talent."

"Your habit."

He stood with her at the threshold. The two of them looked into the dark room without going in.

"Is everything in order?" he said.

"Everything is ready."

"Bon."

A silence. He had a way of letting silence ask a question for him.

"Erik," she said, because she could feel him about to ask. "He arrived. I have him on the floor. He answered a press question without bleeding. He will be at the toast."

"He came down."

"He came down."

"Then we are well."

"Father."

"Mm."

She kept her voice operational; she had learned, somewhere in her thirties, that operational was the only voice he could not interrupt.

"Is there anything from — last winter — that I should be ready to answer for, if Mr. Crane goes looking?"

His face did not change. He looked, instead, at the dark above the bed.

"Last winter," he said, "was put in order long ago. There is nothing to resurrect, my dear. Some things keep, and some things do not; the things that did not keep, I had the kindness to put away."

He did not look at her as he said it.

"Bon," she said.

A chime sounded along the corridor — the soft three-note tone the staff used to draw the principals to the dais. Below them the orchestra modulated.

"Go down," he said. "I will follow. I want a moment with the room."

"Father —"

"A moment, Adriana."

She closed the suite door with the same hush it had opened on, locked it, and went down the corridor toward the stair. Behind her, in a corridor that had been built in a year and tested for eight months and inspected by men in white shirts with grease pencils, the empty Coral Suite stood unopened above an unbroken party.

---

Erik slipped the toast.

He did it the way he did everything, by being elsewhere on a believable errand, and the errand he chose was the Southwest Observation Gallery because the Southwest Observation Gallery was the one room of the Aurelia he had stood in only on paper. Fifteen yards of curved acrylic seven inches thick, the deepest single span of clear material in the building, looking out across the dropoff. He had lost an argument about its mounting flange in 1977. He wanted to be alone in it for ninety seconds before Vauclair called the room to attention and made him stand.

The gallery was nearly empty. Two couples at the far end, voices lowered the way voices lower in a chapel, and a steward at the threshold with hands folded. Erik nodded to the steward, walked the curve, and stopped halfway along.

The dropoff began ten meters out and went where it went. The floodlamps along the gallery's exterior ribs cut a wedge of blue through the dark; in that wedge silver fish, a school of them, drifted the way the ocean drifts things that do not need to hurry. Beyond the wedge, in the dark that the lamps did not reach, larger shapes moved without committing to a direction. The acrylic was very cold under his palm. The pressure outside it — he did not let himself calculate the figure; he had calculated it enough — pressed against the glass with the patience pressure has, and the glass held, and he leaned his forehead briefly against it and let the cold come into the bone above his eye.

When he straightened, the angle of the floodlamp seam glare changed across the inside surface, and he saw it.

A pale hairline. Maybe four inches. Curved very slightly, off the vertical, midway up the curve of the panel, in the seam-side third where the acrylic met the steel. He went still.

He took a step to the left, then a step back, the way you step around a painting in a gallery to be sure of what you are seeing, and the line moved the way a reflection moves and not the way a flaw moves. A hair caught in the lamp. A polishing scratch. A trick of the seam. He counted, in his teeth, three, and let himself put it where such things go: cosmetic. Some installer's last-day glove had brushed the panel at the wrong angle. The Aurelia was four months old. Buildings carried these the way new shoes carried scuffs.

Behind him the service door opened, and Tomás Reyes leaned half through it, a clipboard against his chest, his uniform whites pressed hard at the cuff and softer at the elbow where he had been working.

"Comandante," Reyes said. The word came out at half volume; he used it like a key, fitted only when no one else was close enough to hear.

"Tomás."

"You found my gallery."

"I found *a* gallery."

"It's mine tonight. I have the watch." A small smile. "I have *all* the watches."

Erik nodded toward the curve. "Pretty room."

"Yes." Reyes did not look at the curve. He looked at Erik's face. "You should be up there."

"I'm going."

"Now, Comandante."

"Now."

The clipboard slipped a little when Reyes turned to hold the service door for him, and Erik, in the way one reads the spine of a book one's friend is carrying, read what was on the top sheet without trying to. Sector codes. Times. *SW Gal.* in Reyes's small upright hand, a dash, *prev. maint. — TR.* The next line was already half-written and he did not see it.

"Tomás."

"Yes."

"Anything I should know?"

Reyes's eyes went, very briefly, past Erik to the curve of acrylic and back.

"Go to the toast," he said. "We'll talk after."

"Tomás."

"After, Comandante."

He said it the second time the way men do when they have noticed they have said it once.

Erik went.

---

The Atrium had filled. Vauclair stood at a low dais near the foot of the helical stair with a glass in one hand and the room in the other. The orchestra had stopped. The applause for nothing in particular had stopped. Adriana stood three steps back of him with her clipboard hugged across her chest the way another woman might hug a child. Erik took the position they had left for him and looked at the polished marble between his shoes.

Vauclair began as he always began, with gratitude. He thanked the investors by group and not by name. He thanked the operations staff. He thanked the Coast Guard for the calm sea. He turned, gracious, and nodded toward Erik and called him "the man who drew this dream," and Erik kept his face still under it. He spoke for perhaps three minutes. He quoted, briefly and without flourish, *La Traviata* — *libiamo, libiamo* — and lifted his glass. The room lifted its glasses.

The lights dipped.

It was the smallest thing. The chandeliers on the dome ribs went, for one full second, to perhaps two-thirds of their throw; the warm white slid toward warm yellow; somewhere behind a wall a relay made the small, definite, unhappy sound a relay makes when it has been asked to do a thing it can do only just. The orchestra's first violin, holding a note for Vauclair's toast, scraped a quarter step flat. A red pilot lamp behind the bar — the kind of lamp guests are not meant to see — winked once and went dark again.

Then the light came back. All of it. Cleanly.

Vauclair had not lowered his glass. His smile widened the necessary half-inch.

"The Atlantic," he said, "is reminding us whose house this is. *Salute.*"

The room laughed, a little, and drank, a little, and forgave. The orchestra came back in clean. A waiter who had frozen with a tray of coupes resumed his line. Adriana, behind her father, made one small mark on her clipboard without looking down at it.

Erik did not drink.

He stood with the glass at his lip and the toast unswallowed and the cold at the bone above his eye where he had leaned against the acrylic, and he thought — without thinking it in words — that the quality of the returned light was wrong. It was too clean. The chandeliers had come back at exactly their previous setting and not at the slightly-off setting that lights come back to after a dip. He could not have said this aloud, and he did not, and the room around him was already restored.

He set the untouched glass down on a passing tray and let the bodies of the room break around him, and he walked, slowly and without seeming to walk, back across the Atrium to the archway of the southwest corridor. He did not call it a return. He told himself he was looking for Reyes. He went on past the archway and down the short corridor and stopped one pace short of the gallery threshold.

From there the curve opened to his eye in full. The blue-white wedge of floodlight beyond the acrylic. The school of silver fish, still in the wedge, fins working harder than they had been working before.

The pale line was not where he had left it.

Not by much — a finger's width, perhaps two. He could not be sure he was remembering correctly. He could not be sure the lamp angle was the same. He could be sure only that he had stood in front of that panel for ninety seconds, and counted it, and that what he was looking at now was not the same.

Beyond the glass, in the wedge of floodlight, the suspended particles in the water were no longer drifting. They were sliding, all of them at once, sideways across the lamps — the whole field of them, the whole lit cone of water, moving laterally in a slow shear that the school of silver fish had already turned into. The fish were holding their position against it, fins working, the way fish hold against a current that has come up on them suddenly.

Erik had no equation for it.

He did not call out. There was no diagnosis to call. The room behind him had just been told, by the man whose building this was, that there was nothing to be alarmed about, and the room had agreed; to break that agreement now, with nothing in his mouth but a wrongness in the light and a line he could not measure, would be to do violence to a hundred people under glass, and he had done enough violence by signing what he had signed.

The orchestra had recovered. Behind him, somewhere at the edge of his hearing, Vauclair was laughing at something the senator had said. Somewhere further behind, on the upper deck, the unlit suite he had passed on the way down kept its ceiling open to the dark water.

Erik stood very still and looked at the water moving wrong across the lamps, and he counted, again, the lamps in the dome ribs above him — sixteen, two banks of eight, the number was correct — and for the first time since they had laid the keel he did not believe the number meant what the number said.
