# Chapter One
## The Aurelia

Inside the elevator-pod the guide cable made a steady tuning-fork tremor through the deck plates, a hum Erik Halvorsen had been feeling in the soles of his shoes for the last three hundred feet and was beginning to feel in his teeth. Through the acrylic of the forward port the Atlantic stood as a sheet of black ink, lit only where the pod's own floodlamps reached, the lamp cones drifting with suspended silt that fell like snow in reverse. The cabin lights were turned low for the descent — Vauclair's idea, ten years old now, that the building should reveal itself to its guests the way a stage came up out of darkness. The man behind him kept saying *Look, look* in a low astonished voice to the woman pressed against the rail. Erik did not look at her. He looked at the building.

It rose out of the floor of the ocean as he had drawn it — wider at the base than the eye accepted, the radial fins braced into the seabed, the dome a calm geometry of acrylic panels and ribbed steel, the helical staircase already visible inside as a slow line of moving lights. The upper crown, that arrogant little balcony of suites perched on top of the dome's shoulder, came level with the descending pod at the count of seven. Erik counted. Six suites lit, the curtains drawn back so that the windows showed warm interiors — a vase of yellow chrysanthemums, a turned-down bed, a silver tray set with two flutes. The seventh was dark. Inside the dark suite there was nothing to see at all, only the pale gleam of an acrylic ceiling reflecting the floodlamps as the pod slid past. The paper that lived against his ribs inside his coat, folded once and once again into a stiff small rectangle, had been written in a different room in a different year. The third one. The one he had not given to anyone. The pod seated itself into the receiving collar with a velvet sigh of seals and a kiss of brass against brass; a green lamp blinked twice above the inner hatch; the cabin lights came up.

The hatch opened on the Aurelia, and warm air pushed past his face, salted with the perfume of women who had dressed for it: tuberose, hair lacquer, a high white-rose note from somebody at the back. The orchestra was playing something Italian and brisk, eight or ten musicians under the apex of the dome, their music rising and folding under the curve of the glass and coming down softened. Brass fittings shone. Crystal moved on trays. The atrium opened in front of him as a single beautiful room and three stories of room above it, the helical staircase climbing the inside of the dome like a slow ribbon. The acrylic of the dome was clean as new ice. Beyond it the floodlamps stood in pale columns through the water, and Erik, in the half second before he was recognized, was a man looking up through his own drawing at the Atlantic Ocean and feeling shame for how much he liked it.

"Mr. Halvorsen."

The press woman had been waiting at the rail. She had a *Continental* badge and a notebook and she pronounced his name with care.

He took her hand without remembering to want to.

"Ma'am."

"How does it feel?"

He counted to three. "Cool. They've got the air right."

She laughed politely, the laugh of a woman with a deadline. Behind her two flashbulbs went off at once and Roland Vauclair came out of the second flash with both hands raised in a small benediction, the smile he had worn in the marketing photographs and the smile he had worn at the Princeton dinner in 1971, unchanged.

"My architect," Vauclair said. He took Erik by both shoulders, briefly, the way one took a son returned from school. "*Mon cher.* You came down."

"I came down."

Vauclair held him a half second longer than necessary. There was no challenge in it; that was the point. He turned, light on his feet for a man his size, and his eye caught on the woman who had come down in the pod behind Erik. She had stopped at the threshold of the atrium and gone pale, her gloved hand on the brass rail and the orchestra and the upturned faces too much for her at once. Erik knew the look. He had seen it on a foreman at Kárahnjúkar who could not, for one bad minute, walk back across a steel span he had walked four times that morning.

Vauclair did not call attention to her. He stepped past Erik as though he had remembered something charming he meant to say, came alongside her at the rail without seeming to arrive, and bent his head so that his words passed only between them.

"Madame. The first time, everyone forgets to breathe. Sí, *comme moi.* Take my arm to the staircase — there, you see, the orchestra, and the lamp under the third step is gold leaf, my daughter chose it — and the floor will be steady under you in twenty paces. *Voilà.*"

He gave her his arm. He walked her three steps, slowly, into the music. He did not look back to see who had seen.

Erik, who had seen, lowered his eyes to the polished floor. The shame in his coat pocket pressed quietly against his ribs.

---

By the time he had crossed half the atrium he was already tired of his own face. The press understood the building first and the architect second and he had spent the better part of three minutes being photographed with his own dome behind his head. He drifted toward the bar against the curve of the south wall and a man in a dinner jacket too well-cut for the room intercepted him without seeming to.

"Halvorsen. Lewes, *Architectural Record.*" A friendly face; a careful question waiting behind it. "Forgive me. One small thing for the record before they take you up to the dais. When you left the project in seventy-seven — was that the resignation that *Engineering News* called creative differences, or were those creative differences in the structural sense?"

Erik counted the bubbles rising in the man's glass. He counted four.

"Every project of this scale has differences," he said. "Creative is one of the words for them."

Lewes smiled. Erik thought he had given him enough, and not enough to print cleanly.

Vauclair, having delivered the woman to the staircase, had circled back through the room with the unerring radius of a man who had felt the question before it was asked. He arrived at Erik's elbow without a sound, plucked a glass of champagne from a passing tray, and turned the conversation as though pulling a tablecloth from under it.

"Mr. Lewes, you must come up to the gallery before dinner. The light at this hour on the southwest is — well. *La luce, la luce.*" He pressed a glass on the journalist. "And you must speak to my daughter about the staircase. She has opinions she did not get from me." A look passed across his face, small and warm. "She has been the building's voice these last six months. The press has been listening to the wrong Vauclair."

Lewes laughed, accepted the redirection, accepted the champagne, drifted. Vauclair turned back to Erik with the smile already softened to private use.

"You hate them, *mon cher.* Drink something."

"I'm fine."

"You are tired and famous, which is exhausting. *Allons.*" Vauclair touched him on the arm and looked past him for a half second to where his daughter had come down the helical staircase in a black gown and was moving toward them through the crowd, a slim clipboard against her hip the way another woman might have carried a fan. He raised his hand. "Adriana, *ma fille* — a moment."

She arrived without slowing. "Papa."

"The reporters." Vauclair leaned in. He spoke in the voice he used for service instructions, flat and quick, English with the French underneath. "They want everything. The old Cape office files, the old change orders, the carbons. *Mon Dieu,* the carbons." He shook his head, smiled at nobody in particular. "Tell Henri to keep them out of the back corridor. Most of that paper is smoke now anyway, but they will not know that, and they will write the smoke."

"Henri knows."

"He knows because you told him."

"Then he knows." She had not looked up from the clipboard. She made a small mark with a pencil. "Daniel is using the service-route gangway again."

"He is faster."

"He is faster because he beats the orchestra to the room. I have told him twice."

"Tell him three times, *chérie,* and he will tell you he is faster."

Behind her, on the cue of his own name, Daniel Park went past at a half-trot with an empty tray balanced on three fingers, the brass cuff buttons of his new white jacket bright with newness, a boy proud of the way the wool sat on his shoulders. He caught Adriana's eye on the way through, grinned at the floor, and was through the service door before she finished the mark on the clipboard.

She gave her father one of the looks Erik had not earned the right to read and went past them both into the crowd. Vauclair watched her go with an expression that did not entirely arrive on his face.

"She runs it better than I do," he said, to no one. Then, to Erik, with a different voice: "Stay near the dais. They will want you for the toast."

He moved away.

Erik stood with his untouched glass and looked up into the slow circling of the staircase. The folded paper at his ribs had warmed to his body. The room was very beautiful. He had loved it once on paper and he was beginning, against his will, to love it now.

---

Twelve paces from the press orbit, behind a pillar dressed as a coral column, Felix Crane found the only quiet inch in the atrium and stood inside it with a tumbler of soda water and a writer's headache already beginning behind his right eye.

He had been working the room for thirty minutes. He had what he needed for the polite version of the piece — Vauclair had given him a usable line about Verdi, the architecture editor would forgive him the rest — and he had what he was beginning, with a writer's slight dread, to suspect he had actually come for: a room a half degree off true. The architect did not want to be photographed. The owner's daughter walked the floor with the rhythm of a foreman, not a hostess. The owner himself had told a small clean lie to the *Architectural Record* and made it taste like a compliment.

The cassette recorder lay in his inside breast pocket, the size and weight of a paperback novel, snug against his ribs through the tuxedo wool. He had not turned it on. He had loaded a fresh cassette at the surface platform and put the spare in the other pocket and he had decided, in the elevator-pod, that the room would tell him when to press the button. The room had not told him yet.

He let his eye drift along the bar. The Florida senator was there, hair perfect, holding a Scotch he had taken a single sip from. He had asked Crane, in a courteous southern voice that had remembered Crane's first name from the briefing folder, what the depth was. The depth, Crane had told him, was four hundred and twelve feet. *Lord,* the senator had said, with the kind of awe that softened him. *Four hundred and twelve.* Three minutes later a waiter had come past with a tray and the senator had not stepped back into him — had stepped, instead, a careful half-pace aside, and laid his hand on the boy's elbow in the small steadying way of an uncle. He had asked the depth again as the boy moved on. Crane had told him again. He had nodded twice and forgotten it again, comfortably.

At the south curve of the bar the older Italian couple from the second page of the briefing folder stood close together, both in black, both very still in the way of people for whom rooms had stopped being new forty years ago. The wife — Marchetti, he was almost sure, the Milanese shipping name — wore a silver pendant at her throat, a small lifeboat in profile no bigger than a thumbnail. It caught the orchestra light, once, and was gone behind her glove. The husband bent his head to hers; she said something that did not move her mouth much; he smiled.

Nearer, at the rail, a young couple — six months married at most, on the husband's face — was being served by a waiter who had not yet had time to learn which of them did the talking. The wife was small and dark and very still. The waiter offered the tray. She covered her glass with her palm and said *No, thank you, just water,* the words held too level, and the husband, who was already turned toward the staircase to point out the lamp under the third step, did not turn back.

Crane catalogued. Crane catalogued and did not press the button.

He set the tumbler down on the bar. The recorder slid in his pocket as he turned, and he laid his palm flat against his ribs to still it, a gesture he had made a hundred times in a hundred rooms and which had so far never gotten him into trouble.

---

The staff corridor smelled of oil-warm metal and floor wax, and the music came through the bulkhead in a softened lower register, the bass viols only. Erik went down the staff stair two at a time, the way one did in a building one had drawn. The pressure of the room at his back lifted off him within ten paces. By twenty he could think again.

Tomás Reyes was in the engineering overlook in his short-sleeved white shirt and his silver-stripe trousers, leaning over a console of analog gauges with a clipboard against his hip and a pencil behind his ear. He did not turn at Erik's footstep. He had heard him at the stair.

"Comandante."

"Tomás."

"You went up?"

"I went up."

"And?"

"It is beautiful."

Reyes made a small Cuban sound that was not quite a laugh. "*Que bueno.*" He tapped a needle with a knuckle — pressure differential at the manifold, steady at green — and made a small mark on the sheet. "We are running clean. Trim is even, the ballast is reading what it should read, the lower deck pumps cycled twice since service opened. The kitchen is using more freshwater than I told them they could. Miss V. has spoken to them."

"They listened?"

"They will."

Erik leaned on the rail and looked down through the overlook glass into the working level. Below, two men in coveralls moved between the pump array under the soft red of the maintenance lamps, the way one moved in a chapel. He had not, he understood now, expected to like the back of the building this much.

Above the console, pinned to the cork board between the duty roster and a laundry chit, a teletype slip carried the standing range advisory for the Hatteras sector — naval surface gunnery, mariners to keep clear, six hours' notice required — yellowing at the corners and tacked through twice as though it had been there since the line was first opened. Erik's eye crossed it and went past. Routine paper.

The wardroom door was wedged open against the heat. On the small steel desk by the door the night's running log lay flat under the lamp, ruled in Reyes's careful hand. Erik's eye caught it because his eye caught paper of that kind by reflex. The line for the early shift had been initialed and crossed. The line under it carried, in pencil:

*Preventive maintenance — southwest service corridor — TR.*

Reyes pulled the log toward himself, dropped his pencil in the spine, and closed it without comment.

"Adriana wants you back for the toast," he said.

"I know."

"She told me to tell you and to send you up the front stair, because if you come up the back stair the photographers will lose you again."

"She told you that."

"She wrote it."

Erik straightened. His coat moved against his ribs and the folded paper inside the lining made its small stiff resistance. He felt Reyes's eyes on the side of his face, not unkindly, for the second it took him to put his weight back on the heel of his shoe.

"I'll go up the front," he said.

"*Buena suerte,* Comandante."

He came up by way of the southwest gallery because he could not help himself. He told himself he was taking the long way back because the air in there was better. He knew, before his hand was on the door, what he was doing. He had drawn the gallery in 1972. He had not yet stood inside it.

It took his breath in a small clean way that embarrassed him.

The acrylic curved out from floor to overhead in one continuous wall, lit from outside by the building's own floodlamps so that the Atlantic stood beyond it in a deep cobalt color veined with paler currents, the lamp cones reaching out fifteen, twenty meters and feathering into darkness. A school of small fish hung in the second lamp's beam, too far to count, turning together. Inside the cones, suspended silt drifted downward in its own slow time, particle after particle settling out of the water column the way dust settled in an empty room. Two guests stood at the rail and did not speak. The acrylic was warm to the touch where his palm grazed it.

He walked the gallery slowly because the building deserved it. At the midpoint, where the curved panel met its neighbor along a bonded seam, a pale hairline traveled six inches up from the brass mullion shadow and stopped. He stopped with it.

His professional eye undertook its small involuntary work before he could prevent it. The line was thin as a hair laid across the acrylic; it could have been a polishing scratch; it could have been a casting artifact from the panel's manufacture. He bent slightly. The line ended a finger's width below a tiny etched inspection tick that the fabricator had punched into the surface during commissioning, a mark no guest would ever see. Below the tick. Not at it. Not past it.

He registered the position. He did not name what it might be. The room above was waiting.

He went up the front stair.

---

The toast gathered them under the dome the way the orchestra had gathered them at the start: by drawing breath together in a high glass room. Vauclair stood on the dais beneath the central chandelier, white-headed, broad-shouldered, his glass in one hand and the other flat on the rail, and the room came up to him the way an audience came up to a tenor in the second act. Photographers crouched. Cameras whirred. Adriana stood at the foot of the dais with her clipboard tucked under her elbow and her face turned three quarters toward her father, the way a stage manager watched a curtain.

"Mesdames et messieurs." Vauclair's voice carried under the dome without effort. "Honored guests. Senator. Gentlemen of the press." He took them in turn, smiling. "I have been told by my publicist that I am to speak for four minutes. I have been told by my daughter that I am to speak for two."

The room laughed.

"Therefore I will speak for three, and we will all forgive each other."

He began to speak about the sea. He spoke as he always did, in long elegant English with the French breathing under it, about glass and salt and what a building was for, and he was very good at it. He named the engineers; he named the divers; he named the cooks. He named, with one open warm hand turned toward the bar, the architect who had given the building its bones — *my friend, my colleague, the co-author of this house,* the words coming out in the order Vauclair always put them in — and the room turned toward Erik and applauded, and Erik did the only thing he could, which was incline his head and lift his glass an inch.

He was lifting it an inch when the lights went down one stage.

It was not a blackout. It was the small clean step a theatre took going from the third act to the curtain call, the chandelier under the dome dimming a quarter and the wall lamps along the bar dimming with it, and somewhere above the dais a red lamp Erik did not remember designing came on for less than a heartbeat and went off again. A glass at the second table rang against a knife as somebody's hand moved. The orchestra lost half a bar. One violinist did not, and held the room together with a single steady note while the others found her.

Vauclair on the dais raised his free hand.

"Ah," he said. "*La diva.*" He waited a beat for the laugh he knew the room would give him and the room gave it to him. "Even our lights have a sense of timing. *Va, pensiero* — the lights wish me to be brief." He raised his glass to the orchestra; the orchestra found the rest of the bar. "To the Aurelia. To the sea. To everyone in this room."

The chandelier came back up. The wall lamps with it. The wine returned to the conversation it had been having, smoothly, as if it had been gone only for breath. A woman near the rail laughed too loud and was forgiven.

Erik did not drink. He had counted, on the way down, four seconds of dimming and one blink of red. He set his glass down and turned toward the southwest gallery before he had given himself permission to turn.

The crowd was thicker now along the south wall; he came at the gallery from the inside arc, the wrong way, and had to step around two men in dinner jackets standing closer to each other than the room required, their lapels carrying the cigar smoke of the surface platform like a memory. He came around the brass mullion of the gallery and looked.

The lamp cones still stood out in the water. The acrylic was warm. The bonded seam ran where it had run. The pale hairline traveled up from the mullion shadow, six inches as before, and then beyond.

It had crossed the inspection tick.

The fresh white point of it, no longer than the nail of a finger, caught the nearer floodlamp directly and held the light a moment, the way a wet edge of paper held light. He bent without bending. He counted the distance from the mullion in case he had misjudged before and he had not misjudged before. The line had moved.

Beyond the acrylic the Atlantic was doing what he could not parse. The suspended silt in the floodlamps' beams, which a quarter hour ago had drifted downward in its own slow time, was sliding across the beams now in a steady lateral set, particle after particle traveling left to right across the lamp cones as though the water itself had turned a slow shoulder against the building. There was no surge in it. There was no current he had built the gallery to expect. It was only the ocean moving sideways across his lamps in a direction he had no equation for, and the chalk-white tip of the hairline catching the light above the tick where it had not caught the light before.

Behind him, under the dome, somebody finished the toast for Vauclair and the room applauded a second time, fuller and easier than the first, the orchestra coming up through the applause with a confident swelling figure that meant *we have recovered, we have been gracious about it, we are going in to dinner now.*

Erik stood with his hand not quite touching the acrylic. He counted, in Norwegian, under his breath, because he was alone enough in the curve of the gallery to be allowed it. *Én. To. Tre.* The warmth of the folded paper at his ribs had not changed. The line in the glass had.

He did not name it. He had not earned the right to name it yet. He only knew, with the small ugly certainty of a man who had drawn the room he was standing in, that something in the building between the toast and the applause had quietly become other than what it had been, and that no one in the atrium behind him had been given any reason at all to know.
