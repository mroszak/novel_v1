# Chapter Two
## Charlotte and the Echo

The coffee in Beckwith's mug had been cold for an hour, and the mug stood inside its metal ring on the chart-table shelf with the small unhappy tremor of a vessel running too quiet for its own machinery. Whenever a man in soft-soled shoes crossed the plate behind him, the ring chimed against the cup, once, faintly. The rest of the time it was still.

He stood with both hands flat on the rim of the table. Beneath his palms a tracing-paper plot lay tacked under four brass weights, the bearings to a faint, unhopeful contact ruled in by the quartermaster of the watch in the waxy beige of a number-two grease pencil and refreshed every four minutes with a fresh dot and a fresh line, the dots walking south-southwest along the plot in a slow, considered hand. The hooded green lamp over the table threw the table's edge in shadow. Above the table the boat's own steady ticks reached him through the soles of his shoes — the small clicks of pipes under temperature, the breathing of the trim pump, the occasional remote thump of a man's heel in the forward passage — the sounds of USS *Charlotte,* SSN-693, three weeks out of New London, running at five knots and one hundred fifty feet off the continental shelf east of Cape Hatteras.

The quartermaster of the watch stood at his right shoulder with the grease pencil held against the seam of his trousers like a man holding a candle in a chapel. They had not spoken in seven minutes.

"Bearing two-four-eight, sir," the man under the headphones said over the sound-powered circuit. The boy's voice came through the bulkhead pickup with the clean economy of a sonarman who had been taught to keep his throat out of the microphone. Cole. Third class. Twenty years old next month, or the month after; Beckwith had signed the chit.

"Range estimate."

"No range, sir. Faint."

"Classification."

A pause measured in his teeth.

"Possible submerged, sir. Not merchant."

Beckwith did not move his hands from the rim of the chart table. He let the breath out through his nose so that the quartermaster would not register him exhaling. *Not merchant* was not, by the manual, a classification. It was the absence of one. It was a young man telling his captain, in the only language the watch bill allowed him, that the noise had not behaved like a screw he had ever been given the chance to learn.

"Hold the bearing," Beckwith said. "Walk it as he gives it to you. Tell Mr. Ennis I want him."

"Aye, sir."

The 21MC popped once, softly, and went out. The deck plates kept their small steady song under his boots.

He let his eye slide off the plot for the half-breath it took to remember the rest of his patrol. The local-operations packet lay folded on the corner of the table under a glass paperweight, the brass corners of the manila already brightening where the watch had thumbed through it. Hatteras gunnery, mariners-keep-clear. A Coast Guard exercise window already closed. A typewritten civilian-coordination sheet on the bottom of the stack, three days old, run through some harbormaster's mimeo and sent down the chain because somebody at the Yard had decided the boat ought to have it: a list of fixed installations in the operating area, charted positions, surface-platform call signs, the kind of housekeeping paper a captain glanced at and signed and filed.

He had glanced at it twice this week. There was an installation south of his patrol box at four hundred and twelve feet, marked on the chart with the soft hexagonal symbol the cartographer used for an obstruction not yet a wreck. He had read the line under the call sign because his eye read lines under call signs the way it read fuses on a panel.

*Routine preventive maintenance — southwest service access — civilian operations active this period.*

He had not slowed at it. Civilian paper produced civilian language at industrial volume. He had signed the sheet and pushed it under the paperweight, and the symbol on the chart was a hexagonal smudge of grease pencil that nobody had inked over yet because nobody had needed to.

Ennis came up the ladder from below and stopped two paces from the table with his hand still on the rail. The exec's khakis carried the heat of the forward passage and the faint smell of a man who had been drinking burnt coffee in the wardroom.

"Skipper."

"Cole has something."

"Heard the call."

"Tighten the boat."

"Aye, sir." Ennis spoke past Beckwith's shoulder to the chief of the watch without turning his head, in the half-voice the control room used when nobody had announced anything yet. "Ahead one third, depth one-fifty, rig for ultra-quiet, secure the dishwasher and the trash compactor. Word to the galley."

"Word to the galley," the chief repeated, and the order traveled out through the boat in 1979 fashion, mouth to ear and ear to switch, a pump dropping out under the deck plates as a man somewhere in the engine room laid his hand on a valve. The coffee mug stopped trembling in its ring. The plate beneath Beckwith's feet fell quieter by a barely perceptible step, as though somebody three rooms away had closed a door.

Ennis came up beside him at the table. He looked at the plot, at the small walking dots, at the grease-pencil hexagon south of the patrol box, at the dots' direction of travel. He did not speak. He had been Beckwith's exec for fourteen months and had learned, by now, that his captain measured time with his hands flat on a table.

"Two-four-seven," the circuit said.

The bearing had stopped wandering.

Beckwith took his right hand off the rim of the table and laid two fingertips on the plot where the dots had begun to bend, and the quartermaster, without being told, brought up a fresh pencil and put a small clean tick beside them and waited.

"Sir," Ennis said, low.

"Yes."

"That isn't shadowing."

Beckwith counted, behind his teeth, to four. Outside the hull the Atlantic carried no information that he had not been given by a frightened twenty-year-old through a headset. He took his hand off the plot and laid it back on the rim.

"No," he said. "It isn't."

* * *

Inside the padded shells of the headphones the ocean had a temperature. Cole had told nobody this, because he could not say it in any language the watch bill would have allowed him to use. The Atlantic at thirty fathoms east of Hatteras was a warm rushing nothing through the band Charlotte's hydrophones gave him; the deep was a colder nothing under it; the screws of merchantmen, when they came in, came in like cars passing on a wet highway one valley over. He had learned the screws of merchantmen first, the way a boy learned dogs. He had been on the boat eleven months. He had learned a great many dogs.

He had not learned this.

He kept his elbows tucked against his sides on the high stool in the sonar shack because the shack was small enough that elbows mattered. The reels of the broadband recorder turned in the cabinet at his left knee with the patient, slightly damp sound of magnetic tape under tension. The amber glow of the bearing-rate indicator threw a soft tongue of light onto the front of his dungarees. His pencil was a stub. His worksheet had a fresh column ruled across the top in the careful printing the chief liked: TIME, BEARING, BEARING RATE, CLASS, CONF. The CONF column was where a man with a manual put a percentage. Cole had been writing dashes there for ten minutes because no honest dog had walked into his ear.

He pressed the soft cup of the right headphone a little tighter against his temple. Sweat had begun to bead at the cartilage. The sweat did not bother him. The thing he was listening to bothered him.

A merchantman put a foot down. That was the only way he could say it. The shaft slap of a merchantman fell on the back of his ear like a man crossing a wooden floor in another room of the house. This was not that. This was a held breath. He had been listening to a held breath for ten minutes. The bearing had crept and crept and then, three minutes ago, had stopped creeping in a manner that had nothing in it of a ship caught in a current.

The boy in him wanted to call it. The third class in him kept his pencil over the column.

"Bearing two-four-seven," he said, into the lip mike, which had a foam sock that smelled, after eleven months, faintly of him. "Steady. Confidence low."

The captain's voice did not come back to him through the bulkhead. He had not expected it to. The captain had heard him and now the captain was standing inside one of his own silences, which Cole had not yet had to be inside of for very long but knew, by reputation, were considerable.

Then, in the headphones, the held breath made a small mechanical mistake.

It was a tube transient. He had heard tube transients on training tapes and he had heard one transient, an American one, on the boat's own door cycle two weeks ago in a drill that had embarrassed nobody. This one was not American. It came in at the right pitch and the wrong duration, and the slap of the door against its stop had a denser resonance than the Mark 48 tube doors did, like a fist on a thicker wall.

His pencil hand stopped. His other hand, of its own accord, came up and pressed both headphones flat against his head as though the sea were a station fading in and out and he were trying to hold a voice.

In the half-second between the tube transient and the next thing he heard, Cole understood, with a clarity that had nothing to do with anything anyone had ever taught him, that the contact had been waiting for him to slow down and that he had given it what it wanted.

The next thing he heard came through the band the manual taught him to know without thinking: the hiss-pop of a screw spooling up under pressure, the high faint whine that was a torpedo's own propulsion taking its first water, the bearing already opening from the parent.

His mouth was dry. His pencil moved exactly where the manual said it should.

"Conn, Sonar," Cole said, in the voice he used on his sister, the one that did not break. "Torpedo in the water, bearing two-four-five, transient, classify hostile."

He let the button go. He pressed it again before the conn could speak.

"Closing."

* * *

The 21MC popped. Beckwith heard the boy's first sentence with his hand already moving.

"All ahead flank. Right full rudder. Make your depth four hundred feet. Launch countermeasures, port and starboard."

The orders left him in the procedural cadence Charlotte's bill had drilled into him in a hundred dawns, evenly spaced, no syllable louder than another. The chief of the watch repeated each phrase. The helmsman repeated it after the chief. The deck heeled under his boots as a man two compartments away spun a wheel he had been raised to spin.

The plot under the brass weights slid an inch as the boat came over.

"Countermeasures away," somebody said.

"Make turns for flank."

"Turns for flank, aye."

The boat lifted into power he had been husbanding. Beckwith's coffee mug came out of its ring and went over the side of the chart table and broke against the deck plate behind his right boot, the porcelain skidding into the kick-rail in three pieces. Nobody bent for it. The pencil rolled across the plot and stopped against a brass weight.

"Bearing two-four-five, closing," Cole said. The boy's voice had gone flat, the flatness of a boy who had stopped trying to hide that he was afraid. "Range — gating — sir, gating now."

Beckwith laid one hand on the back of the quartermaster's neck for a half second, a thing he had not done before to that quartermaster and would not do again, and took it away. He turned his face into the talker's microphone.

"All hands, brace."

The talker repeated it on the 1MC for the men who could not hear the conn.

The hit came in not as the blow Beckwith had been trained to fear but as the lesser blow of a weapon that had been seduced ten yards off the boat's flank by the countermeasure he had launched and a depth he had bought with two seconds of full rudder. It detonated forward of the beam. Charlotte rang. She rang as a steel building rang when something heavy fell off a higher floor, the ring of her transmitted through her own bones into his teeth and out through the deck plate against the side of his boot, a long high note that lasted longer than it had any right to and then was not there. The chart-table lamp jumped out of its socket and the green hood swung on its cord and the plot pencil rolled off the edge.

Battle lamps came up red in stages, a sunset arriving inside a winter afternoon.

A repeater above the helm went out and stayed out. The trim gauge on the bulkhead behind him fell out of its frame on one side and hung from its wires. Somewhere aft a man yelled the way men yelled when seawater touched skin that had been dry a second ago.

"Damage Control, Conn," Beckwith said, into the talker. "Report."

He had to say it twice, because the first time the talker's microphone had a thumb of his own breath against it. The second time the talker put it through.

The sound-powered phones had a sound of their own on the after circuit, a small dry crackle behind every word, like grit between two stones. The damage-control officer's voice came through it without preamble.

"Conn, DCC. Flooding, frame fifty-two area. Bulkhead four. Hatch shut, sir, hatch shut."

Beckwith took the words in the part of his mind that took such words. He understood, before the second sentence, what *hatch shut* had cost. There were men forward of bulkhead four who had been at their stations when the order to brace had reached them. There were men forward of bulkhead four who were no longer at their stations and no longer reachable. The hatch shut because a chief had spun a wheel against the pressure of seawater pushing the other way, and the wheel had gone home, and the chief had done what the bill said and what nobody in this room or any other room aboard this boat would ever ask him to talk about.

Beckwith did not let himself stop on it. He let himself feel, only, the small cold pressure of his own thumb against the rim of the plot where the grease-pencil dots had stopped walking.

"Hold the bulkhead," he said. "Pumps to four. Damage control party to frame fifty."

"Pumps to four, aye."

"Conn, Sonar." Cole again. He had not been knocked off the circuit. "Contact opening, sir. Bearing two-five-zero, drawing right."

He was reporting it the way the manual said. Beckwith felt for him, briefly, in the way a man felt for another man inside the same fall.

Ennis was at the chart table. The XO had taken the bracing somewhere on his right kneecap; he stood with his weight on his left foot and his right hand flat on the plot. His face under the red battle lamp had the color of paper.

"Skipper."

"Yes."

"Fire control reports tubes one and two ready, sir. Solution good on the contact." A half-second. "He is opening. If we are going to do it, sir."

Beckwith looked at the chart and at his exec and at the boy under the red lamp at the back of the room who had picked up a piece of his coffee mug from the deck and was holding it without knowing he was holding it.

He let the half second be a full one. Some quieter part of him had already stepped back to stand at his shoulder and watch the rest of him do what the rest of him was about to do. He noted the standing. He set it aside.

"Tube one," he said. "Shoot on generated bearings."

Ennis did not move. Beckwith had not, in fourteen months, given Ennis an order Ennis had needed a half second to put through. The half second came now and went.

"Tube one. Shoot on generated bearings, aye."

The exec turned and passed it to fire control through the talker, in the same voice in which he had ordered ultra-quiet eight minutes before. Tube one fired. The boat shouldered. The plate under Beckwith's boots took the weapon's first push and gave it back. Somewhere forward, on the other side of a hatch a chief had closed, men were on the wrong side of seawater that Charlotte's pumps were beginning, very slowly, to win against.

He did not move his hand off the rim of the chart table for a long count. The pumps on bulkhead four made their honest dirty sound under the plate. The red battle lamp threw the chart in the color of meat. He kept his eyes on the smudge and did not look away.

* * *

The Soviet captain had a thumb on the lip of the depth gauge and the gauge would not settle. It had been settling reliably for six years of his command and it was not settling now. Under his thumb the lacquer of the dial had been polished to a faint clouded place by the gesture of years; he kept his thumb there now without meaning to, a man's thumb against a wound. The needle drifted toward two hundred meters and away again as though the boat could not decide.

The lamps in the command compartment had gone amber two minutes ago and were going lower in steps. He had ordered countermeasures launched at the moment the screw of the American weapon had come in clean on the bearing his sonar gave him, and the countermeasures had launched, and the boat had turned, and the boat had not turned fast enough.

The American weapon struck aft of his beam.

He had thought, until that second, that he understood what a torpedo did to a boat. He had been at three exercises with live charges in the Baltic, he had read the after-action reports of the Northern Fleet, he had stood in the same compartment as the engineers who built the pressure hulls of his own class. The understanding he had carried into this minute was the wrong understanding. The blow shook the dial under his thumb and his thumb came off the lacquer and the gauge spun off the pin and back, and then the lights went down to one row of battery emergency, the dim red running line that lay along the overhead like the inside of a throat, and the noise of his own boat reached him as the noise of an animal in a room with him that he had not known was in the room.

Something tore aft of the reactor space. He did not need a damage report. It came to him through the bulkhead as one heard a thing through a wall in an apartment building, a piece of furniture falling over and going on falling. The deck angle came up under his boots and stayed up. He took a half step to keep his balance and put a hand against the periscope shaft for the rest.

His first officer was at the trim panel with his cap off, his hair gone wet. Two men he had not yet learned to call by the names their mothers had given them were at the helm.

"Blow the bow," he said, in the language of his service and his life. "All ahead, both shafts, plot the angle for me, *starpom* — read me your gauges, talk to me."

They talked to him. The boat answered them as an animal answered a man it had decided to forgive but not to obey. The bow came up partway and stopped. Aft, the long tear made its second sound — a longer sound, a piece of structural metal opening along a seam under pressure, the sound of cold seawater meeting hot oil and not making any agreement either could honor — and then, in the bulkhead behind his right shoulder, a panel of gauges went dark together, six dials at one stroke, as though a hand had passed across them.

He let his other hand off the periscope shaft. He took a quarter step toward his first officer. He did not say what he was thinking, because what he was thinking belonged in a letter, which he would not write tonight, because he had begun to understand that he would not be in a place to write a letter tonight.

The lamp by the chart table guttered out. The amber row over the helm held.

"Casualty in the after compartment," somebody said. The voice was a stranger's, very young.

"Seal it," he said. "Seal everything aft of the reactor. Read me the angle. Read me the *angle.*"

The first officer read him the angle. The angle did not move.

He took his hand from the periscope shaft and he placed it, palm flat, against the cool steel of the bulkhead beside the dead gauges, a man with no other authority left to him placing a hand on the wall of his own house.

Outside, in the black water that he could not see, parts of his boat began to leave it. He understood, without being told, that they would not come back.

* * *

In the red light of Charlotte's control room, with the chart-table lamp swinging on its cord and the deck plate against Beckwith's boots full of the laboring of the bulkhead-four pumps, the sound-powered phone at the captain's left ear made the gritty sound it had been making since the explosion and brought him, after a long minute, Cole's voice again.

"Conn, Sonar."

"Conn."

"Multiple hard contacts, sir. Spreading from the parent."

"Bearings."

The boy gave him bearings. He gave them in pairs, in the discipline of a sonarman trained to walk new contacts across the band as they separated from the source. The bearings climbed across the same arc Charlotte's solution had used. They were not climbing alone. There was a slow southwest set under them, a drift that any man who had spent a winter off Hatteras would have known the look of, and the contacts were riding it.

Ennis had come back to the chart table with a damp grease pencil between his fingers. The trim gauge still hung from its wires behind them. The exec laid his left hand flat on the plot and his right hand laid down a fresh bearing line from Charlotte's mark and walked it out into the open water south-southwest of them, and the line crossed the small hexagonal smudge that had been there all afternoon, the smudge the cartographer had used for an obstruction not yet a wreck.

The smudge sat on the line.

The exec did not say anything for the length of time it took him to draw a clean circle around it with the grease pencil. The pencil was wet and the circle smeared at the closing.

"Skipper."

"Yes."

"That's an installation."

Beckwith looked at the smear. He looked at the bearings climbing across the plot. He looked, behind the smudge, at the typewritten line he had read this week and not slowed at. He pulled the local-operations packet out from under the paperweight and the paperweight rang against the brass corner of the table and he opened the packet to the civilian sheet without taking his eyes off the chart.

He read the line again. He read the call sign and the depth and the housekeeping language. He read the words *southwest service access* and did not look up.

"Conn, Sonar." Cole was still on the circuit. Nobody had ordered him off it. "Bearing two-four-six and two-five-one and two-five-three, sir, all opening, all walking south-southwest. Drift consistent."

"Sonar, Conn." Beckwith laid the packet flat on the chart and put his thumb on the line. The grease pencil from the wet circle had transferred to the side of his hand without his noticing. "Stay on them. Walk every contact. Range estimate where you can."

"Aye, sir."

Ennis had not lifted his hand from the chart. The exec's voice when it came was the voice he used to read written orders aloud, low and even and meant for a record.

"That installation is occupied tonight, sir. Civilian operations active."

Beckwith looked at him. Ennis did not look up from the plot.

The sound-powered phone in Beckwith's hand made its small dry crackle. The open circuit had carried Ennis's words too. Cole's voice came back through the bulkhead at the same young, procedural pitch he had used when the torpedo went into the water, and the word he gave the conn was the word the exec had used.

"Civilians," Cole said.

Beckwith laid his thumb flat on the chart and the wet grease pencil smeared at the edge of the circle Ennis had drawn. The pumps on bulkhead four worked in the plate under his boots. The red battle lamp threw the smudge into something darker than the chart paper around it. He pressed the talk button.

"Sonar, Conn. Say again."
